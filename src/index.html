Calculations

This section documents every calculation performed by the analyzer. Each subsection follows the order in which the code evaluates results: drum geometry first, then line tension, then drive performance (electric or hydraulic), and finally dynamic/inertial loads. Every equation shown here corresponds directly to a line of code in the computation engine.

A) Drum Geometry and Cable Spooling

The drum is modeled as a cylindrical core with a Lebus liner, onto which cable is wound in discrete wraps organized into layers. The geometry determines how much cable fits on the drum, the effective winding diameter at each layer, and — critically — the moment arm that converts line tension into drum torque.

Wraps per layer. The number of wraps that fit side-by-side across the drum face is set by the flange-to-flange width divided by the cable diameter. The result is truncated to the nearest half-wrap (Lebus grooves allow half-wrap crossovers). On even-numbered layers the wraps shift by a half-pitch, so one fewer wrap fits when the calculated value is a whole number.

$$W_{\mathrm{layer}} = \mathrm{trunc}_{\,0.5}\!\left(\frac{F_{f2f}}{d_c}\right)$$

layer-engine.mjs:72

First-layer (bare drum) diameter. The innermost winding surface sits on top of the Lebus liner, so the effective diameter of the first layer is the core plus twice the liner thickness plus one cable diameter (the cable centerline is one radius above the surface):

$$D_1 = D_{\mathrm{core}} + 2\,t_{\mathrm{lebus}} + d_c$$

layer-engine.mjs:80

Layer diameter growth. Each subsequent layer adds cable on top of the previous one. In a Lebus-groove arrangement the cables do not stack directly on center — they nestle into the valley between wraps below. The packing factor (typically ~0.877 for round cable on Lebus) captures this geometric nesting:

$$D_{\ell+1} = D_{\ell} + 2\,d_c\,\phi$$

layer-engine.mjs:128

Wrap length. Each wrap is one full circumference at the current layer diameter, converted from inches to meters:

$$L_{\mathrm{wrap}} = \pi\,D_{\ell} \qquad (\text{in inches; } \times\,0.0254 \text{ for meters})$$

layer-engine.mjs:102–103

Spooled and deployed lengths. The total cable length is the sum of operating depth and dead-end reserve. As each wrap is added, the spooled (on-drum) length accumulates and the deployed (hanging) length is what remains:

$$L_{\mathrm{cable}} = z_{\mathrm{operating}} + z_{\mathrm{dead\text{-}end}}$$

layer-engine.mjs:68

$$L_{\mathrm{spooled}} = \sum_{i=1}^{w} L_{\mathrm{wrap},i} \qquad L_{\mathrm{deployed}} = L_{\mathrm{cable}} - L_{\mathrm{spooled}}$$

layer-engine.mjs:108–117

B) Line Tension

The static line tension at any point during deployment is the weight hanging below the drum: the payload at the end of the cable, plus the weight of all cable that has been paid out. As the winch spools out cable, deployed length increases and tension rises linearly with depth.

$$T(z) = m_{\mathrm{payload}} + w_c \cdot z_{\mathrm{deployed}}$$

utils.mjs:49

where is the submerged payload weight (kg-force), is the cable weight per unit length in water (kgf/m), and is the length of cable hanging below the drum (m). This value is computed for every wrap position.

C) Drum and Drivetrain Torque

Line tension acts at the winding radius to create a torque demand on the drum. This torque must be delivered through the gearbox and ultimately by the prime mover (electric motor or hydraulic motor). The torque scales with the layer radius — outer layers demand more torque for the same line pull.

Drum torque. Converting line tension from kgf to newtons and multiplying by the layer radius:

$$\tau_{\mathrm{drum}} = T \cdot g \cdot r_{\ell}$$

analysis-data.mjs:81

where m/s² and (layer radius in meters).

Gearbox torque. The torque seen at the gearbox output shaft (between gear stages G1 and G2) is the drum torque divided by the number of motors and the second gear ratio. This is the value checked against the gearbox rating:

$$\tau_{\mathrm{gearbox}} = \frac{\tau_{\mathrm{drum}}}{N_m \cdot G_2}$$

analysis-data.mjs:87

Motor torque (electric). The torque at each individual motor shaft is the drum torque divided by the full drivetrain multiplication:

$$\tau_{\mathrm{motor}} = \frac{\tau_{\mathrm{drum}}}{G_1 \cdot G_2 \cdot N_m}$$

analysis-data.mjs:90

D) Electric Drive — Speed and Tension

An electric drive is speed-limited by two independent constraints: the motor's maximum RPM (set by the VFD or nameplate rating), and the power available from the motor. The winch can never exceed either limit, so the available line speed is the lesser of the two.

Power-limited motor RPM. Given a motor's shaft power and the torque it must deliver, the maximum RPM it can sustain is found from :

$$\omega_{\mathrm{power}} = \frac{P_{\mathrm{motor}} \cdot 60}{2\pi\,\tau_{\mathrm{motor}}}$$

analysis-data.mjs:93

where (watts per motor). At low tension the motor can spin very fast; at high tension it slows down.

Line speed from motor RPM. Motor RPM translates to line speed through the gear reduction and drum circumference:

$$v = \pi \cdot D_{\ell} \cdot \frac{\omega_{\mathrm{motor}}}{G_1 \cdot G_2} \qquad (\text{m/min, with } D_{\ell} \text{ in meters})$$

utils.mjs:62–66

Available line speed. The power-limited speed and the max-RPM speed are each converted to line speed, and the minimum governs:

$$v_{\mathrm{avail}} = \min\!\big(v_{P},\; v_{\mathrm{max\text{-}RPM}}\big)$$

analysis-data.mjs:105

Available line tension. The maximum tension the electric drive can exert is determined by the motor's peak torque rating, amplified by the full gear train:

$$T_{\mathrm{avail}} = \frac{\tau_{\mathrm{motor,max}} \cdot G_1 \cdot G_2 \cdot N_m}{r_{\ell} \cdot g}$$

utils.mjs:54–59

E) Hydraulic Drive — Speed, Pressure, and Tension

A hydraulic drive has three independent constraints on line speed: the total flow the pumps can deliver (flow-limited), the total electrical power driving the pumps (power-limited), and optionally a hydraulic motor RPM cap. The available speed is the minimum of these. Unlike an electric drive where torque is the primary motor-side variable, hydraulic systems are governed by pressure and flow.

System flow. Each pump string delivers flow based on the electric motor speed and pump displacement. Total system flow is the sum across all strings:

$$Q_{\mathrm{string}} = \frac{\delta_{p} \cdot \omega_{\mathrm{e}}}{3785.41} \qquad Q_{\mathrm{total}} = Q_{\mathrm{string}} \cdot N_{\mathrm{strings}}$$

utils.mjs:69 → analysis-data.mjs:54–55

(3785.41 cc/gal converts cc/min to US GPM.)

Flow-limited hydraulic motor RPM. The flow available to each hydraulic motor determines its maximum speed, capped by its nameplate RPM limit:

$$\omega_{\mathrm{m,flow}} = \min\!\left(\omega_{\mathrm{cap}},\;\frac{Q_{\mathrm{total}} \cdot 3785.41}{N_m \cdot \delta_{m}}\right)$$

utils.mjs:70 → analysis-data.mjs:127

Flow-limited line speed. The hydraulic motor RPM is geared down and converted to line speed at the drum:

$$v_{Q} = \pi \cdot D_{\ell} \cdot \frac{\omega_{\mathrm{m,flow}}}{G_1 \cdot G_2}$$

analysis-data.mjs:128

Power-limited line speed. The total usable electrical power sets an upper bound on line speed. Power equals force times velocity, so dividing the available power by the line tension gives the maximum speed the power plant can sustain:

$$v_{\mathrm{power}} = \frac{\mathrm{HP}_{\mathrm{e\text{-}motor}} \cdot N_{\mathrm{strings}} \cdot \eta_{\mathrm{e\text{-}motor}} \cdot 745.7}{T \cdot g} \cdot 60$$

analysis-data.mjs:132–137

(The factor of 60 converts m/s to m/min; converts kgf to newtons.)

Available line speed.

$$v_{\mathrm{avail}} = \min\!\big(v_{P},\; v_{Q}\big)$$

analysis-data.mjs:141

Required pressure. The hydraulic pressure needed to generate the required torque at each hydraulic motor is derived from the displacement-torque relationship. This tells you how much of the system's pressure capacity is being used at each wrap position:

$$\Delta p = \frac{\tau_{\mathrm{h\text{-}motor}} \cdot 2\pi}{\delta_{m} \times 10^{-6}} \cdot \frac{1}{6894.76}$$

utils.mjs:72–76 → analysis-data.mjs:125

where and 6894.76 Pa/psi converts to psi. The operating pressure is capped at the system maximum .

Available torque and tension from max pressure. At full system pressure, the torque each hydraulic motor can deliver is:

$$\tau_{\mathrm{h\text{-}motor,max}} = \frac{\Delta p_{\mathrm{max}} \cdot 6894.76 \cdot \delta_{m} \times 10^{-6}}{2\pi}$$

utils.mjs:78–81 → analysis-data.mjs:60–63

Multiplied through the gear train and all motors, this becomes the drum torque capacity, and dividing by the layer radius gives the maximum line pull:

$$T_{\mathrm{avail}} = \frac{\tau_{\mathrm{h\text{-}motor,max}} \cdot G_1 \cdot G_2 \cdot N_m}{r_{\ell} \cdot g}$$

analysis-data.mjs:119–122

Hydraulic power consumed. At the available speed, the actual flow and pressure used determine hydraulic horsepower consumed. The code works backwards from the available line speed to find the drum RPM, then motor RPM, then the flow each motor requires, and finally applies the standard hydraulic power formula:

$$\mathrm{HP}_{\mathrm{hyd}} = \frac{\Delta p_{\mathrm{operating}} \cdot Q_{\mathrm{used}}}{1714}$$

utils.mjs:83 → analysis-data.mjs:150

The electrical input power is then , both capped at the system's total usable capacity.

F) Dynamic Loads — Inertia and Available Acceleration

When the winch must accelerate or decelerate (as during active heave compensation), the drive must supply extra torque beyond what static tension demands. The available acceleration margin depends on how much torque the drive can deliver above the static load, and on the total rotational inertia of the drum and cable.

Cable moment of inertia. The cable wound on the drum is modeled as a thick-walled cylinder with inner radius equal to the core (plus Lebus liner) and outer radius equal to the current layer radius. Using the hollow-cylinder formula:

$$J_{\mathrm{cable}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,m_{\mathrm{cable}}\!\left(r_{\mathrm{layer}}^2 + r_{\mathrm{core}}^2\right)$$

analysis-data.mjs:171–172

where (mass of cable currently on the drum, in kg).

Total inertia.

$$J_{\mathrm{total}} = J_{\mathrm{drum}} + J_{\mathrm{cable}}$$

analysis-data.mjs:173

is the bare drum's rotational inertia (from SolidWorks or measurement), in kg·m².

Available drive torque. The maximum torque the drive can deliver at the drum comes from whichever drive type is enabled. For an electric system it is the motor peak torque multiplied through the gear train; for a hydraulic system it is the torque at maximum system pressure. Only the enabled drive contributes — if electric is off, its torque is zero and vice versa:

$$\tau_{\mathrm{elec,drum}} = \tau_{\mathrm{motor,max}} \cdot G_1 \cdot G_2 \cdot N_m \qquad (\text{electric enabled})$$

analysis-data.mjs:177

$$\tau_{\mathrm{hyd,drum}} = \tau_{\mathrm{h\text{-}motor,max}} \cdot G_1 \cdot G_2 \cdot N_m \qquad (\text{hydraulic enabled})$$

analysis-data.mjs:178

$$\tau_{\mathrm{avail}} = \max\!\big(\tau_{\mathrm{elec,drum}},\;\tau_{\mathrm{hyd,drum}}\big)$$

analysis-data.mjs:179

In practice only one drive type is active at a time, so equals the torque from that drive. If both were enabled simultaneously, the greater would govern.

Torque margin. The surplus torque above what static tension requires — this is what's available to accelerate the load:

$$\tau_{\mathrm{margin}} = \tau_{\mathrm{avail}} - \tau_{\mathrm{drum,static}}$$

analysis-data.mjs:180

Available linear acceleration. The angular acceleration of the drum converts to linear acceleration at the cable through the layer radius:

$$\alpha = \frac{\tau_{\mathrm{margin}}}{J_{\mathrm{total}}} \qquad a = \alpha \cdot r_{\ell}$$

analysis-data.mjs:185–186

This value is computed for every wrap. The layer tables report the minimum across all wraps in each layer — the worst case for that layer. If the torque margin is zero or negative, the drive cannot accelerate at all and .

G) Wave Kinematics — Required Velocity and Acceleration

For active heave compensation (AHC), the winch must match the vessel's heave motion. The vessel heave is modeled as a sinusoid with wave height (peak-to-peak) and period . The required line velocity and acceleration are the first and second derivatives of this sine wave:

$$z(t) = \frac{H}{2}\sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi}{T_w}\,t\right)$$

$$v_{\mathrm{max}} = \frac{\pi\,H}{T_w}$$

plots/wave-contours.mjs:391

$$a_{\mathrm{max}} = \frac{2\pi^2\,H}{T_w^2}$$

plots/wave-contours.mjs:85

The contour plots in Section 2 show these values across a grid of wave heights and periods, overlaid with horizontal lines showing each layer's available speed or acceleration. Where a layer line falls below a contour, the winch cannot fully compensate the heave at that sea state.

H) Torque Compliance Checks

The electric drive section computes compliance checks against component torque ratings. These verify that the gearbox and motor are not overloaded under the worst-case operating condition or the Factory Acceptance Test (FAT) load.

FAT drum torque. The FAT condition is 125% of the rated safe working load (SWL) at the full-drum diameter (outermost layer, maximum moment arm):

$$\tau_{\mathrm{FAT}} = T_{\mathrm{SWL}} \cdot 1.25 \cdot g \cdot \frac{D_{\mathrm{full\text{-}drum}}}{2} \cdot 0.0254$$

electric.mjs:237

Operating peak drum torque. The maximum drum torque encountered during normal operation across all layers:

$$\tau_{\mathrm{ops}} = \max_{\ell}\!\big(\tau_{\mathrm{drum,max,}\ell}\big)$$

electric.mjs:239

Design worst-case. The torque used for compliance is whichever is greater — the FAT load or the operating peak:

$$\tau_{\mathrm{design}} = \max\!\big(\tau_{\mathrm{FAT}},\;\tau_{\mathrm{ops}}\big)$$

electric.mjs:242

This design torque is then divided down through the gear train to find the torque at the gearbox and at each motor, and compared to their respective ratings.
